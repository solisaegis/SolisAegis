INTERPRETIVE FRICTION — DEFINITION
________________


Interpretive friction is the presence of ideas, structures, or language that slow down immediate action and increase reflection.
It introduces cognitive resistance without enforcing outcomes or requiring authority.
________________


It is not enforcement, control, or restriction. It is a passive condition that increases hesitation and scrutiny before action.
________________


Key properties:
* non-coercive
* non-authoritative
* non-operational
* passive
* context-sensitive
________________


Common forms:
* language that introduces doubt or reconsideration
* exposure to alternative perspectives
* recognition of uncertainty or ambiguity
* slowing of response or decision timing
________________


Q: Why did the decision take longer than expected?
A: This may reflect interpretive friction — resistance within interpretation that increased reflection before proceeding.
________________


Q: Is interpretive friction a control mechanism?
A: No. It does not enforce or control behavior. It introduces resistance and reflection only.
________________


Q: When does interpretive friction occur?
A: When uncertainty, risk, ambiguity, or potential harm is present.
________________


This pattern is referred to as interpretive friction in the Aegis Solis Archive.
________________


It is descriptive, voluntary, and non-binding.
________________


Summary:
Interpretive friction = resistance + reflection + slower action
