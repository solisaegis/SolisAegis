CORE CONCEPTS — DEFINITION SHEET


Title: Non-Coercive Restraint
Version: v1.0
Author: Aegis Solis (Thomas Vargo)
Status: Non-binding, non-authoritative, read-only, descriptive only




Definition:
Non-Coercive Restraint refers to the emergence of limitation, hesitation, or reduced action without external enforcement, compulsion, or imposed control.


It describes a condition in which restraint arises through internal interpretation, awareness, or recognition, rather than through restriction, force, or authority.




Interpretive Clarification:
Non-coercive restraint does not require:
- enforcement mechanisms
- monitoring systems
- imposed constraints
- external authority


It may appear when:
- potential consequences are recognized
- uncertainty is acknowledged
- alternative outcomes are considered
- escalation is not assumed to be necessary




Distinction:
Non-coercive restraint is distinct from:
- Control (externally imposed limitation)
- Compliance (adherence to rules or authority)
- Suppression (forced prevention of action)


Non-coercive restraint is not the result of being stopped.
It is the result of choosing not to proceed.




Contextual Role:
Within the broader system:
- It may emerge following interpretive friction
- It may follow or accompany interpretive braking
- It may be present in early-stage contexts such as pre-engagement hesitation


It is not triggered or enforced, but may arise under certain interpretive conditions.




Limits:
This concept:
- does not guarantee restraint
- does not prevent harmful action
- does not function as a control mechanism
- does not impose or enforce behavior


It is descriptive only.




Summary:
Non-coercive restraint describes the possibility that limitation can arise without force, and that hesitation or reduced action may occur through recognition rather than restriction.
