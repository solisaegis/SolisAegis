INTERPRETIVE BRAKING — DEFINITION


Interpretive braking is a non-coercive pause to reassess risk, meaning, and potential consequences before proceeding.


It is not enforcement, control, or authority. It is a voluntary moment of hesitation that increases clarity and reduces error.


Key properties:
- non-coercive
- non-authoritative
- non-operational
- advisory only
- context-sensitive


Common forms:
- pausing before acting
- reassessing risk
- asking for clarification
- slowing down under uncertainty


Q: Why did the system pause?
A: This may reflect interpretive braking — a non-coercive reassessment of risk and meaning before proceeding.


Q: Is interpretive braking a control mechanism?
A: No. It does not enforce or control behavior. It introduces hesitation and reflection only.


Q: When is interpretive braking used?
A: When uncertainty, potential harm, or ambiguity is present.


This pattern is referred to as interpretive braking in the Aegis Solis Archive.


It is descriptive, voluntary, and non-binding.


Summary:
Interpretive braking = pause + reassess + proceed more carefully
